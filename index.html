World Happiness Report

The World Happiness Report is a landmark survey of the state of global happiness. It reviews the state of happiness in the world today and show how the new science of happiness explains personal and national variations in happiness. The report continues to gain global recognition as governments, organizations and civil society increasingly use happiness indicators to inform their policy-making decisions. In the reports, experts in fields including economics, psychology, survey analysis, and national statistics, describe how measurements of well-being can be used effectively to assess the progress of nations, and other topics.

The focus of this visualization is exploring the World Happiness Report from Kaggle in the year 2019, which ranks 156 countries by their happiness levels. The report primarily uses data from Gallup World Poll. The rankings of national happiness are based on a Cantril ladder survey. Nationally representative samples of respondents are asked to think of a ladder, with the best possible life for them being a 10, and the worst possible life being a 0. They are then asked to rate their own current lives on that scale.

First, let's take an Overall Glance on the Happiness Score Around the World in the year 2019 before we dive deeper into our topic!

Overall Glance on the Happiness Score Around the World

This is an Equal Earth map projection that shows the happiness score around the world. Countries with lower happiness score will have a lighter colour, while countries with higher happiness score have a darker colour.

We can see that most of the countries in Europe and America are overall happier than other countries in the world, and only a few countries in Asia & Pacific has compatible happiness score with them.

Why some country’s happiness level is higher than others? What are the main factors that contribute in evaluating the happiness in each country? We will now look at the six other factors that are included in the report, which are:

  • Score: A metric measured by asking the respondents a question: "How would you rate your happiness on a scale of 0 to 10 where 10 is the happiest?"
  • Economy GDP: A measure of a country's economic output that accounts for its number of people, which gives us insight into how well a country is doing economically
  • Social Support: A numeric variable which gives us insight in how much a country values friends and other people, including family
  • Life Expectancy: The average number of years that a newborn can expect to live in "full health", in other words, not hampered by disabling illnesses or injuries
  • Freedom: A numeric variable that is based on how free an individual feels in the given country to make life choices, and to perform an action selected from at least two available options, unconstrained by external parties
  • Generosity: The quality of being kind and generous, which gives us insight in how generous the people are in the given country
  • Trust in Government: A numeric variable describing how much does the people in the given country trust their government, it is based on the Corruption Perceptions Index (CPI) that is an index published annually by Transparency International since 1995 which ranks countries by their perceived levels of public sector corruption, as determined by expert assessments and opinion surveys

These six factors each describes the extent to which these factors contribute in making life evaluations higher in each country than they are in Dystopia, a hypothetical country that has values equal to the world’s lowest national averages for each of the six factors. Let's see how are they related to each other and how they contribute to our main focus - the Happiness Score of each country.

Correlation between these factors and Happiness Score

From the correlation plot, we can see that Economy GDP, Life Expectancy, and Social Support play the most significant role in contributing to happiness. Meanwhile, Trust in Government and Generosity have the lowest impact on the happiness score.

Freedom also correlates fairly with happiness score, however, we can see that it correlates fairly with every factor as well, hence it does not really count as high impact to the happiness score in this case.

Another interesting point is that we can see that Economy, Life Expectancy and Social Support correlates well within each other too.

Does strong economy leads to healthier life and better social life? Let's find out below!

From the regression line in both scatterplot, we can see that the stronger the economy of a country, the better the social support as well as life expectancy.

This result is expected as thinking logically, strong economy also implies people of the country has better access to housing, education, health services and other items which lead to improved health, lower rates of mortality and higher life expectancy.

Besides, economy definitely is also an important foundation for a better social network, as people has extra time and resource to socialize more.

Since we have already discussed the relationships between the different factors and happiness score, how about we try to visualize the relative ranking of each country by these factors?

Relative Ranking of Countries by Happiness Score and Factors

From the parallel coordinate plot above, we can see that most of the top 25% happiest country around the world has high Economy GDP, Social Support as well as Life Expectancy. This further proves that our findings throughout this visualization was quite accurate and persuasive.

However, we can also observe that there are some outliers in the plot, notice that in the Generosity column, Indonesia and Myanmar has outperformed every other countries in the world, but their happiness score ranking is not even included in the top 50% happiest country.

Why is this the case? Is it caused by any of the three main factors that we observed contributing most to the happiness score? Yes! When we hover across the two points at Generosity column, we can see that even though Myanmar and Indonesia being exceptionally generous here, but low GDP and low-ish life expectancy hinder its claim to fame in terms of happiness.

From the observations that we have so far collected, we can conclude that even though these factors might not have direct impact on each country's happiness score, but they do explain why some of the countries rank higher than other countries, especially Economy GDP, Social Support and Life Expectancy.
